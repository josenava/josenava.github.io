Switching from VPS to a rock64 server

September 2, 2018    VPS rock64

DISCLAIMER: I am not expecting more than 100 concurrent requests, so I believe I don’t need everything a VPS can offer.

I owned a VPS with OVH which was extended yearly since 2015. As the time to renew it again was approaching this year, I decided to cancel my contract and stop renewing the VPS as the only usage I was giving to it was hosting a small blog with no visits which I created to learn a little bit about Wordpress.

Here you can see the cost of renewing the VPS since 2015.

Great Jose you are going to save 30 euros this year, what now? Let’s spend that money on a credit card sized server!! well, and a little bit more :D. By the time of renewing the server I owned a raspberry pi but I felt it was not going to be powerful enough and also would lack of ram in case I wanted to do several things at the same time, so I looked for alternatives and found the rock64 with 4GB of ram. This seems like a legit candidate to build my own credit card server..

So after spending ~$72 (~62€) + 10€ on a 32GB sdcard and 2 weeks waiting for the delivery, I got my own server!

Now it is time to gets our hands dirty with our terminal and install an OS. I chose debian as it seemed the most stable choice, although my suggestion is to take a look on the rock64 linux community and pick the one that fits you the most.

After installing my favourite packages it was time to start this blog from scratch (this will be the topic for another post!) and setup my domain to point to my ip address and enable the access to the rock64 as it is under a NAT.

To configure the ip address of the domain I just used ipify and setup manually the ip address on godaddy. However, there is an automatic way to do this with a cronjob so it will take the ip address every time the router is restarted. Maybe I’ll blog about this one day..

Comparing the prices, as currently a VPS costs 36€ a year, it might be a bit expensive as it would take me 2 years to compensate the money I paid for the rock64 but I just wanted to try it and see if it can work as a server. Maybe I’ll need to use it in different ways to take advantage of its power and make it cheaper than a VPS :)

PS: I don’t recommend hosting a business in this kind of devices as if there is a power outage in your apartment the website will be down!!
